Dorothy Vaughan

Without different views and opinions the world of research would become stagnant. By limiting who has the right to education we limit what we can develop. Before machines could do our thinking for us they had to be taught how to think, and before that we relied on human power alone. Some of the greatest scientific leaps in the mid-20th century were devised from human computers.

In 1915 the “National Advisory Committee on Aeronautics” (NACA) was formed in the united states (Macalester 2021) in which the Langley Research Centre was eventually integrated. This is where Dorothy Vaughan joined in 1943 and was assigned to the west wing computers (Spelman College 2016). The west wing computers were an “all-black group of female mathematicians” (Nasa 2017) as during this time there was huge segregation between black and white people in America. Jim crow laws wouldn’t legally be abolished until 1965 so Dorothy and others like her faced huge opposition to get to their positions. In 1940 only 2% of black women held a college degree and of that percentage 56.9% were in teaching roles (McDaniel et al. 2011). The west wing computers, including at one point the acclaimed Katherine Johnson who went on to provide calculations that were used in numerous NACA/NASA missions notably John Glenn’s first human orbit of the earth (NASA 2020), began to both individually and as a group distinguish themselves by contributing to all areas of Langley research (Nasa 2017). She further defied odds by becoming the first black supervisor in NACA where she would hold the position for nearly a decade. Being adept at navigating racial, gendered and social obstacles she furthered equality for everyone as Langley began to removed or ignore “colored” signs through-out the facility (Shetterly 2017) as well as intervening on the “behalf of white computers in other groups who deserved promotions or pay raises” (Nasa 2017). 1958 NACA transitioned into becoming NASA Dorothy saw the opportunities and taught herself and others FORTRAN, in which she became an expert, and became involved in the SCOUT launch program (Nasa 2017).

This is all to say that Dorothy Vaughan didn’t create a new programming language or changed the fundamentals of coding, however she was a great computer scientist, transitioning from human to digital computer work and paved the way for many to come. Her own contributions helped lift science into a new age of development while the legacy she left behind has allowed thousands to follow in her footsteps.
