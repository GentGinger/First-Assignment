Compuational Thinking

The term computational thinking was first penned by Seymour Papert in his book Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas in 1980 (Papert, 1980) but never defines it explicitly. In 2006 Janette Wing brought the phrase back stating “CT involves solving problems, designing systems, and understanding human behaviour, by drawing on the concepts fundamental to computer science” (Wing, 2006). Tabesh (2017) writes that there are fours stages involved in computational thinking that have mostly been agreed upon; Decomposition; Pattern recognition; Abstraction; and Algorithm Design (which are built upon Papert’s original design).

Figure 1: Computational thinking diagram from Tabesh (2017)

  1. Decomposition involves taking a problem and breaking it down into smaller more manageable problems.
  2. Pattern recognition does as it says as the intention it to be able to recognise trends in data and patterns in work.
  3. Abstraction is keeping hold of the relevant information and ignoring information that makes solving the problem more convoluted.
  4. Algorithm Design is planning the steps by which a problem is solved taking the form of a simple order of instructions using pseudocode or a flow chart instead.

Computational thinking will be the basis for all my future coding work. Without writing any code the problem will have to first be broken down into its constituent parts. Patterns, similarities and repetition can be spotted within the tasks to make the work shorter. Going down unnecessary paths and dead ends can be stopped using abstraction. Once the details have been worked out, finally a plan can be made for a step by step solution, and given an algorithm the majority of the work is done it can then be translated into any language needed. Computational thinking isn’t just a tool for a single language, it can be applied to all and every language I attempt and beyond that.
